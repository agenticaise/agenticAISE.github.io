Risks, benefits, and changes of agentic AI in Software Engineering

Related to: Beatrice Melis, Donatella Donati, Marco Autili, Paola Inverardi, Patrizio Pelliccione, Agentic AI in Software Engineering: tools or team members?, ACM Transactions on Software Engineering and Methodology (TOSEM), 2026.

Goal of this living repository

This page summarizes a taxonomy of Agentic AI in Software Engineering along two dimensions: proactivity (Passive, Reactive, Proactive) and interaction role (Questioning, Team-member, Manager). For each category, it provides a curated view of expected benefits, risks, and changes to SE practice.

The repository is intended to be “living”: as new evidence emerges (research results, industrial reports, case studies), the community can propose updates or additions via the companion “Suggest an update” page.

Agent types (from Table 1) — meaning + examples

The examples below are taken from the paper’s Table 1 (“Classification of Agentic AI by behavioral capabilities and organizational role”) and are meant as illustrative instantiations.
Passive & Questioning Agents
PassiveQuestioning

Execute user commands or hardcoded behavior when invoked and engage in interaction with humans to provide a better service.

Example: Code or documentation generators that establish a dialogue to better fit user needs.

Passive & Team-member Agents
PassiveTeam-member

Execute user commands when invoked, and can understand and engage in complex social and interpersonal dynamics of a human team.

Example: Passive support agents triggered to clarify requirements or summarize discussions, adapting communication to team needs without acting autonomously.

Passive & Manager Agents
PassiveManager

Not applicable in the taxonomy: managerial roles require initiative beyond passive, on-demand interaction.

Reactive & Questioning Agents
ReactiveQuestioning

Respond to environmental stimuli using predefined rules or behaviors, engaging with humans to provide a better service.

Example: Reactive CI agents monitoring repository events/build results and applying predefined policies to adjust configurations or trigger corrective actions.

Reactive & Team-member Agents
ReactiveTeam-member

Interactive collaborators that respond to stimuli, ask clarifications, and react to team needs through context-aware communication.

Example: Requirements engineering agents monitoring discussions/issue trackers and reacting to changes by asking clarifying questions and identifying ambiguities or conflicts.

Reactive & Manager Agents
ReactiveManager

Supervisory agents that respond to stimuli by assigning tasks, monitoring progress, and reacting to project events.

Example: Coordination agents that detect delayed tasks/failing builds and reallocate tasks, update sprint plans, notify team members, and escalate issues when needed.

Proactive & Questioning Agents
ProactiveQuestioning

Autonomous agents that observe, plan, and act without explicit triggers; they can also ask clarifying questions when uncertainty arises.

Example: Self-adaptive testing agents that continuously monitor changes and autonomously adapt testing strategies, generating/modifying tests and querying developers when needed.

Proactive & Team-member Agents
ProactiveTeam-member

Fully autonomous teammates that proactively collaborate, adapt to context, and support shared team goals through continuous contributions.

Example: Autonomous development partners that monitor repositories/issues/docs and propose design alternatives, contribute code/tests, update documentation, and initiate discussions to align decisions.

Proactive & Manager Agents
ProactiveManager

Advanced manager agents that plan, coordinate, allocate resources, oversee quality, and manage projects end-to-end.

Example: Project management agents integrating issue trackers/VCS/CI/testing/comms to plan iterations, assign/reassign tasks, enforce standards, monitor quality signals, and coordinate releases with escalation to humans.

Risks, benefits, and changes (from Tables 2–4)

The lists below summarize the paper’s findings for the eight applicable agent types (Passive Manager is not applicable).
Passive & Questioning Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
PassiveQuestioning
Benefits (Improve)
  • Productivity enhancement (time saving, faster improvements in code quality) via on-demand support and micro-level reasoning.
  • Accessibility for novice/less expert engineers; support for learning and skill acquisition; continuous learning and knowledge exchange.
  • Increased motivation, reduced programming anxiety, improved performance; perceived satisfaction and well-being via emotional support.
Risks (Lose)
  • Over-reliance; erosion of independent reasoning/diagnostic skills; superficial learning.
  • Erroneous outputs leading to incorrect learning or no learning.
  • Security and IP risks, bias, data security issues; potential decrease in code quality/maintainability; operational and energy costs.
Changes
  • Education must prepare learners to engage with diverse agent types.
  • Shift toward AI-mediated inquiry/dialogic interaction; increased emphasis on high-level design decisions remaining with humans.
  • Requirements engineering toward prompts; new practices to verify/measure auditability, accountability, fairness, transparency.
Passive & Team-member Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
PassiveTeam-member
Benefits (Improve)
  • Contextual assistance in collaboration when explicitly requested; clarification/synthesis for shared understanding.
  • Mediation across disciplines enhancing inclusivity, efficiency, and creativity.
Risks (Lose)
  • Loss of review culture; misalignment with team practices and conventions.
  • Mediation risks: misunderstandings/oversimplifications or propagation of misinformation.
  • Reduced internalization of knowledge and weaker peer-to-peer learning if team sense-making is delegated to the agent.
Changes
  • Need to train engineers to work with Agentic AI as colleagues (and potentially managers).
  • Changes in learning-by-doing and debugging cultures.
Reactive & Questioning Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ReactiveQuestioning
Benefits (Improve)
  • Event-triggered support for inquiry/reasoning tasks; responsiveness and contextual learning at critical moments.
  • Enables refocus on creative/strategic work; acts as glue between repos/APIs/databases via orchestration; helps maintain shared understanding.
Risks (Lose)
  • Deferral of reasoning until agent intervention; reinforcement of passive cognitive patterns and skill atrophy.
Changes
  • Emergence of event-driven AI support in workflows; agents as integration points for reactive coordination/orchestration.
Reactive & Team-member Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ReactiveTeam-member
Benefits (Improve)
  • Event-driven coordination within socio-technical teams; mediation across roles/disciplines.
  • Reduced collaboration breakdowns; enhanced emotional well-being; refocus on creative work.
  • Collaborative debugging support: accelerates optimization and error correction.
Risks (Lose)
  • Erosion of human-centered collaboration (empathy, informal communication, mentoring) and challenges to sustaining expertise.
  • Marginalization of socially complex debugging; reduced knowledge sharing and situational awareness; increased social/productivity pressure.
Changes
  • Transformation of interaction and information sharing; reduced reliance on traditional learning practices.
  • Need for explicit shared mental models due to reduced situational awareness.
Reactive & Manager Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ReactiveManager
Benefits (Improve)
  • Improved coordination/alignment across teams; cross-disciplinary mediation and operational flow management.
  • Efficiency gains through workload redistribution, freeing developers from routine work while shifting oversight burdens.
Risks (Lose)
  • Surveillance, performance benchmarking, and pressure to conform to machine-driven standards.
  • Fairness/merit attribution issues; potential inequalities/injustice; reduced situational awareness due to insufficient explainability.
  • Accountability risks from AI-mediated managerial reactions and responsibility gaps.
Changes
  • Reorganization of managerial practices toward AI-assisted orchestration; training to work under AI management.
  • Restructuring merit/work distribution (e.g., rewarding systems); need accountability, governance, and explainability frameworks.
Proactive & Questioning Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ProactiveQuestioning
Benefits (Improve)
  • Support inquiry and anticipatory clarification; early intervention before breakdowns.
  • Contextual learning at critical moments; sustained cognitive/creative support; orchestration across tools/artifacts; supports shared understanding.
  • Particularly valuable for novices: enables learning and reduces stress.
Risks (Lose)
  • Marginalization of trial-and-error and reflective learning; passive acceptance of solutions.
  • Skill atrophy and reduced sense-making via over-automation; unclear responsibility for agent-initiated actions.
Changes
  • Acceleration toward AI-driven inquiry workflows; stronger reliance on orchestration and prompt-based interaction.
  • Need for human-centered governance instruments and robustness practices.
Proactive & Team-member Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ProactiveTeam-member
Benefits (Improve)
  • Socio-technical coordination and mediation; emotional well-being; collective learning and creative focus.
  • Improved knowledge sharing/documentation; can foster focused strategic discussions.
  • Tutoring and reflective partnering for novices/professionals.
Risks (Lose)
  • Erosion of human-centered collaboration; loss of collective sense-making and cultural transmission.
  • Over-reliance/deskilling; loss of care structures and emotional support; alienation; increased productivity pressure; diluted responsibility.
Changes
  • Shift toward teaching how to work with agentic colleagues; human–AI pairing revitalizes pair programming.
  • Conversational interaction models; transformed learning practices; explicit shared mental models become more important.
Proactive & Manager Agents — Benefits / Risks / Changes
ProactiveManager
Benefits (Improve)
  • End-to-end planning/coordination; proactive task and resource allocation; early detection of inefficiencies.
  • Improves communication/coordination among humans; efficiency via workload redistribution.
Risks (Lose)
  • Surveillance and productivity pressure; fairness/merit attribution concerns.
  • Opacity reducing situational awareness; accountability risks and responsibility gaps.
Changes
  • Reorganization toward AI-assisted orchestration; training to work under AI management.
  • Restructuring merit/reward systems; explicit governance, transparency, limits, and human oversight.
