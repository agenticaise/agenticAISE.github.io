Final taxonomy and risk-benefit analysis

The final outcome of the work is not only the role- and initiative-aware taxonomy, but also the risk-benefit analysis derived from it. This page reports both: the final taxonomy and the benefits, risks, and expected changes associated with each agent category.

Definition

Agentic AI in Software Engineering

We use the term Agentic AI in Software Engineering to denote AI-based systems that operate with some degree of autonomy beyond isolated tool invocation, interact with humans or other agents, and influence software engineering activities by observing context, making decisions, and acting within socio-technical workflows.

Design choice

Why Passive Team-member is excluded

Although the two dimensions are conceptually orthogonal, not every combination is retained as a meaningful or desirable agent category. In particular, Passive Team-member agents are excluded. By analogy with human organizations, one could argue that a team member may play a passive role while still belonging to a team. However, the taxonomy is not intended to reproduce all patterns observed in human organizations; rather, it aims to support the engineering of agentic systems for SE teams. Systems that only act when explicitly invoked provide useful on-demand support, but they do not meaningfully participate in team coordination, shared responsibility, or collaborative initiative. They are therefore classified as passive support tools rather than team members.

Boundary cases

How to interpret the taxonomy

The taxonomy is not a maturity model and does not imply that organizations should move toward increasingly proactive or managerial agents. Instead, it provides an analytical lens for distinguishing configurations that have different benefits, risks, and governance requirements.

Passive Questioning agents are already common in current software engineering practice. Reactive Team-member agents are retained as boundary cases because they can participate in shared work through event-driven coordination. Proactive Team-member and Proactive Manager agents remain forward-looking and higher-stakes configurations because they can initiate work, influence priorities, and reshape responsibility structures.
