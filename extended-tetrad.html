Extended McLuhan Tetrad & literature grounding

This page brings together the literature material used to scope the work and the extended McLuhan Tetrad construction material. The first iteration produced an 11-paper scoping corpus; the Tetrad construction then expanded the evidence base through targeted searches, snowballing, workshop insights, and additional literature.

Construction material

How this material should be used

The Tetrad is an analytical scaffold used to organize evidence and reasoning about what Agentic AI may enhance, obsolete, retrieve, and reverse in Software Engineering practice. These items are not final empirical claims; they are traceable hypotheses and synthesis categories used to construct the risk-benefit analysis.

First iteration

Scoping literature material

The first iteration searched for secondary studies and related papers that could scope Agentic AI in Software Engineering. This step produced an 11-paper scoping corpus after title screening, full checking, snowballing, and complementary checks of recent SE2030-related literature. The scoping corpus informed the initial taxonomy and open questions.

( "agentic AI" OR "AI agents" OR "autonomous agents" OR "software agents" ) AND ( "software engineering" ) AND ( taxonomy OR survey OR "secondary study" OR review OR "mapping study" OR "empirical study" )

The query was run in Scopus over metadata plus full text, restricted to post-2023 computer-science and AI venues.

Traceability

Source base for the Tetrad construction

The Tetrad construction expanded the evidence base beyond the first-iteration scoping corpus. It drew on the scoping corpus, SE2030 workshop insights, targeted searches, backward and forward snowballing, primary empirical studies, conceptual work, practitioner-oriented analyses, community surveys, and industry reports.

In the long construction material, the extended Tetrad cites 112 unique source items across its four dimensions: 32 for Enhances, 28 for Obsolesces, 27 for Retrieves, and 62 for Reverses. Because some sources support more than one dimension, these dimension counts are not additive. Our current classification identifies 93 academic literature items and 19 additional supporting sources, including books, theoretical sources, practitioner/community material, and industry-oriented evidence.

The source-by-source traceability file lists each reference, the Tetrad dimension(s) in which it is cited, and the reference entry used during construction.

Enhances

What Agentic AI may amplify or intensify

  • Acceleration of development pace and productivity: shorter bounded coding, testing, refactoring, and documentation tasks.
  • Coordination glue in socio-technical ecosystems: connection across repositories, issue trackers, CI/CD pipelines, documentation, and communication channels.
  • Accessibility of development and SE education: conversational support and step-by-step scaffolding for novices and non-specialists.
  • Mediation in cross-disciplinary collaboration: translation between stakeholder language, requirements, code, tests, and architecture.
  • Emotional buffering and frustration prevention: immediate assistance when developers face blockers.
  • Continuous learning and intuition triggering: contextual explanations, alternatives, and clarification questions during work.
  • Cognitive offloading and creative refocusing: freeing attention for architecture, design trade-offs, and higher-level reasoning.
Obsolesces

What may be displaced or marginalized

  • Empathy as a human-centered practice: risk of treating empathy as an interface feature rather than a team competence.
  • Traditional SE educational practices: less manual trial, error, debugging, and incremental design.
  • Collaborative debugging culture: fewer opportunities for peer learning through joint diagnosis.
  • Information and knowledge sharing: less informal human exchange when agents become the central retrieval mechanism.
  • Human-peer mentoring and ad hoc communication: novices may ask agents instead of colleagues.
  • Learning-by-doing and error-based reflection: productive friction may be removed too early.
Retrieves

What may return in transformed form

  • Pair programming: human-AI pair work retrieves pair programming with a non-human partner.
  • Dialogic programming: requirements, design, code, and explanations become negotiated through dialogue.
  • Coding-flow customization and versatility: agent behavior adapts to context, conventions, and preferences.
  • High-level system design and architecture: routine offloading may return attention to system-level reasoning.
  • Agent orchestration and coordination patterns: delegation, monitoring, escalation, and role allocation become explicit design problems.
  • Requirements engineering toward prompts: intent, constraints, examples, and acceptance criteria become central to agent interaction.
  • Validation and verification through practical means: review, tests, traces, and runtime feedback gain importance.
Reverses

What benefits may turn into when pushed too far

  • Diffuse thinking models: responsibility for reasoning becomes spread across humans and agents.
  • Loss of care structures: mentoring, empathy, and belonging may weaken.
  • Social and productivity pressure: speed expectations may intensify.
  • Passive dependence and deskilling: useful support may become over-reliance.
  • Merit and workload distribution: AI-mediated work may complicate attribution and fairness.
  • Shared mental model building: teams may rely on agent summaries instead of building shared understanding.
  • Accountability structures: causal chains become harder to reconstruct as autonomy increases.
